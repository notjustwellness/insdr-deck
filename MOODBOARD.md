# INSDR Apparel — Visual Moodboard

> Reference brands: Y-3, H-O-R-S-E, 1989 Studio

---

## Brand Dissections

### Y-3 (Yohji Yamamoto x Adidas)

**What they are:** The original high-fashion x sportswear collaboration (2002). Yamamoto's avant-garde draping meets adidas' technical engineering.

**Visual Identity:**
- Typographic-only logo — bold sans-serif, no icon, no crest
- Strictly monochrome: black (always), white, charcoal, concrete grey
- Texture over color — when everything is black, differences in fabric finish (matte vs. shine), weight, and construction become the visual language
- Minimal copy. The brand assumes its audience already knows.

**Design Language:**
- Oversized, architectural silhouettes — wide-body, dropped shoulders, elongated hems, asymmetric cuts
- The adidas three stripes are never used conventionally — always subverted: tonal, mesh-rendered, one-sided, deconstructed
- Construction IS the design — stitching, hardware, seam placement do the talking
- Technical fabrics: neoprene, bonded materials, coated cottons, ripstop
- Forward-looking: language references the future, not heritage

**Photography:**
- High-contrast, often monochromatic
- Movement and dynamism — garments shot in motion, never static
- Urban/industrial environments
- Athletes and artists cast together (Noah Lyles + A$AP Nast in SS25)
- Max Vadukul (FW24): "Black-and-white is king... if you want emotion, you can't beat black-and-white"

**What INSDR takes from Y-3:**
- Monochrome as philosophy, not limitation — invest in texture and material contrast
- Typography-only branding — a bold wordmark that gains power through restraint
- Construction as design language — when branding is minimal, the craft speaks
- Subvert the expected — take familiar wellness/activewear conventions and recontextualize them
- Assumed authority — never explain yourself, speak to the informed consumer

### H-O-R-S-E (h-o-r-s-e.net)

**What they are:** Cotton workout essentials. Anti-synthetic, anti-logo, anti-everything-unnecessary. Named after the pickup basketball game.

**Visual Identity:**
- Hyphenated wordmark: H-O-R-S-E — forces deliberate reading, turns a common word into a graphic device
- Dual typography system: Times New Roman (headlines) + Chivo sans-serif (body) — deliberately "un-designed" serif choice reads as intellectual confidence
- Strictly monochrome grayscale — zero accent color, ever. Products provide the only color.
- Aggressive whitespace as a luxury signal

**Design Language:**
- 16 products total. That's it. Extreme curation.
- "THE [descriptor]" naming: The Big Short, The Track Pant — simple, definitive
- 100% cotton, GOTS-certified organic, sourced from USA, Japan, Peru, Portugal
- No visible logo on clothing. Their stance: "Because feeling good as you move your body has nothing to do with us. These clothes are for you."
- $55–$295 price range — premium justified by materials and provenance, not branding

**Photography:**
- Clean, natural lighting on neutral backgrounds
- Named model references: "Cook is 6'1 and wears a Large" — personal, not institutional
- No heavy styling — clothes shown as workout clothes, not fashion editorial
- The garment is always the subject

**Brand Tone:**
- "Cotton Clothes for Working Out" — five words, zero spin
- Nostalgic but not sentimental: "harkens back to a time when physical fitness was simple"
- Anti-corporate: no jargon, no "performance," no "engineered," no "innovation"
- Warm, literate, understated

**What INSDR takes from H-O-R-S-E:**
- The direct overlap is striking: 100% cotton, anti-synthetic, anti-PFA, substance over branding
- Extreme product curation — tight SKU count signals confidence
- No-logo-on-product as a brand position (or near-invisible branding)
- Specific provenance storytelling — where the cotton comes from, who manufactures
- The hyphenated wordmark treatment — I-N-S-D-R could work this way
- Named sizing references for human warmth
- Spotify playlist as a lifestyle touchpoint beyond product

### 1989 Studio

**What they are:** "Grown-up streetwear" — culturally accessible luxury. Late 90s/early 2000s nostalgia filtered through contemporary craft. Founded by Chaz Jordan (worked alongside Virgil Abloh at RSVP Gallery).

**Visual Identity:**
- Clean sans-serif wordmark, all-caps, minimal
- Warm earth-tone palette: burgundy, caramel, chocolate brown, sage green, cream — plus black and white
- More fashion house website than streetwear drop page — reads closer to COS or Acne Studios
- Generous whitespace, grid-based layouts, restrained UI

**Design Language:**
- Relaxed, oversized fits that feel effortless but intentional
- Cotton-first: 100% cotton jerseys, heavyweight cotton tees with micro ribbed crewnecks
- Narrative-driven collections — each season follows "the 1989 man and woman" through life stages
- Cultural/nostalgic graphic treatments that carry meaning (not decorative)
- Anti-trend: every piece designed to be an "instant classic"
- Inclusive sizing to 3XL with custom options — health and fitness are not one-size-fits-all
- Price range: ~$250–$3,000+

**Photography:**
- Cinematic, nostalgic — each collection feels like a film still from a specific era
- Warm, natural lighting
- Theatrical runway presentations (SS24: fictional high school with live skateboarders)
- Models styled as real people with personal style

**Brand Tone:**
- Confident without being exclusive
- Culturally fluent — hip-hop, skate, and luxury referenced with genuine fluency
- "Dishevelled to a degree" — effortless everyday cool
- Aspirational but accessible — explicitly rejects gatekeeping

**What INSDR takes from 1989 Studio:**
- "Grown-up streetwear" is almost exactly where INSDR lives — substance over hype
- Warm earth-tone palette feels more wellness-aligned than stark black-and-white
- Narrative-driven collections — build around wellness journeys, not just seasonal drops
- Cotton-first as a premium material, not a compromise
- Inclusive sizing as a brand value, not an afterthought
- Cultural credibility through authenticity, not sponsorship

## The Cross-Section: Where All Three Overlap

These three brands converge on a specific set of principles. This intersection IS INSDR's apparel DNA:

### 1. Material Integrity as Identity
All three brands let the material do the talking. Y-3 uses texture contrast in monochrome. H-O-R-S-E certifies every fiber's origin. 1989 Studio builds collections on heavyweight cotton. **For INSDR: 100% cotton isn't a constraint — it's the entire design language.** Different weights, weaves, washes, and finishes become your palette.

### 2. Anti-Logo Confidence
Y-3's wordmark is the only mark. H-O-R-S-E puts zero branding on the garment. 1989 Studio keeps logos near-invisible. **For INSDR: the brand is recognized through silhouette, material quality, and construction — not a chest logo.** The INSDR wordmark lives on internal labels and subtle details. People who know, know.

### 3. Construction Over Decoration
All three invest in how garments are built rather than what's printed on them. Stitching, cut, hardware, seam placement. **For INSDR: premium cotton construction is the visible differentiator.** The anti-synthetic stance means the craft has to speak louder.

### 4. Assumed Audience Intelligence
Y-3 never explains Yamamoto. H-O-R-S-E says "Cotton Clothes for Working Out" and nothing more. 1989 Studio references culture without footnotes. **For INSDR: "If you know, you know" extends to the apparel.** Don't explain why cotton matters on the garment — explain it in the content. The clothes just ARE.

### 5. Curated Restraint
Y-3 drops focused collections. H-O-R-S-E has 16 SKUs total. 1989 Studio releases narrative capsules. **For INSDR: launch tight.** A small, intentional collection that's sold out beats a large catalog that sits.

### 6. Typography as the Primary Brand Mark
All three are wordmark-driven. No icons, no crests, no mascots. Bold, clean type that works across garment labels, digital, and packaging. **For INSDR: the "INSDR" wordmark is the mark.** Consider the H-O-R-S-E hyphenation treatment: I-N-S-D-R.

## INSDR Apparel Direction — Synthesized

### Palette
- **Primary:** Deep Evening (#1A1410), Rose Linen (#F6EFEA) — the existing brand palette
- **Signal accent:** #AD4236 — used sparingly (internal labels, stitching detail, packaging)
- **Apparel colors:** Earthy, warm — black, off-white, slate, warm grey, clay. Pull from 1989 Studio's warmth, not Y-3's cold monochrome.
- **Texture over color:** Different cotton weights and finishes (brushed, ribbed, french terry, jersey, waffle knit) create visual variety within a tight color range

### Typography
- **INSDR wordmark:** Bold, condensed sans-serif. Confident. Works at every scale.
- **Consider:** I-N-S-D-R hyphenated treatment for select applications (inspired by H-O-R-S-E)
- **On garment:** Minimal to invisible. Internal label, subtle neck tag, or tonal embroidery only.

### Silhouettes
- Relaxed, oversized fits — Y-3's volume meets 1989 Studio's effortless proportions
- Gym-functional but not gym-coded — no performance seaming, no reflective hits
- Designed to be worn training AND out. The same hoodie works at the gym and at dinner.
- Dropped shoulders, generous body, tapered where it counts

### Naming
- Follow H-O-R-S-E's "THE [descriptor]" simplicity
- The Tee. The Crew. The Short. The Jogger. The Hood.
- No invented words, no codes, no "Pro" or "Elite" suffixes

### Branding on Garment
- Near-invisible externally — the H-O-R-S-E play
- Internal label with full provenance: "100% Cotton. Knitted in Portugal. No synthetics. No PFAs."
- Signal red (#AD4236) on internal labels only — an insider detail
- Tonal embroidery or debossed INSDR on a single detail (hem, cuff, back neck) — visible only up close

### Photography Direction
- Y-3's movement and dynamism + H-O-R-S-E's honest simplicity + 1989 Studio's warmth
- Real gym environments, natural light — not studio
- Athletes and artists together (Vanness bridges both worlds)
- Show the cotton — close-up texture shots, fabric in motion, sweat on natural fiber
- Named fit references: "Van is 5'11 and wears a Medium"

### Packaging
- Matte, tactile, minimal — Y-3's monochrome restraint
- Signal red accent on internal elements only
- Cotton drawstring bag instead of plastic polybag (the anti-plastic message extends to packaging)
- "100% Cotton. 0% Plastic." printed on packaging

## The Signal — Color as Brand Mark

INSDR apparel carries no visible logos. The brand identifier is **Signal (#AD4236)** — applied as a subtle, consistent detail across every SKU. The color replaces the logo.

### The Detail System

| Element | Placement | Visibility | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Signal bar tack** | Bottom left hem, every garment | Exterior — subtle, 6–8mm | The identifier. Spotted across the gym by those who know. |
| **Signal woven label** | Inside neck | Interior — hidden when worn | The provenance mark. "100% Cotton. No Synthetics. No PFAs." |

**Nothing else.** No Signal panels, stripes, or color blocking. Two details only. Restraint is the point.

### Precedent

| Brand | Detail | Lesson |
|---|---|---|
| Maison Margiela | Four white stitches on back collar | A stitch became more recognizable than any logo |
| Bottega Veneta | Dropped all logos; the intrecciato weave + green IS the brand | A texture and a color can replace an entire identity system |
| Hermès | Orange | You see the color across a room and you know |
| Acne Studios | Face patch | Tiny, consistent, unmistakable |

### How The Signal Scales

- **Day one:** Only INSDR wearers know the bar tack. Secret handshake.
- **Phase 2:** "The Signal" becomes shareable content — "Now You Know" posts about the red stitch, what it means, why it's there.
- **Phase 3:** Spotting the Signal in the wild becomes the insider experience. User-generated: "Caught a Signal at the gym today." The community identifies itself through a color, not a logo.
